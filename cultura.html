Cultura

PRODUCT DESIGN, PERSONAL PROJECT

Creating an interactive and time-friendly language-learning experience for busy students.

Role: Research, Wireframing, Usability Testing, Prototyping
Duration: Oct 2019


Learning a new language as a student isn’t easy. I designed Cultura to explore how to create a motivating and accessible language-learning experience for students, centered around verbal communication.

Research > Problems > First Iteration > Second Iteration > Business Considerations > Next Steps


Research

To get a better understanding of the language-learning problem space, I surveyed six university students with non-leading open-response questions.

6/6 used online resources
5/6 consumed media (books, music, tv, youtube videos) to learn
4/6 wished they had a native speaker to help with pronunciation
6/6 used videos/audio to learn pronunciation
6/6 mentioned pronunciation to be the most difficult part of learning

From these numbers, the students value accessibility to low-cost resources and verbal communication. The students’ qualitative answers show the reason they started learning a language in the first place: their cultural ties to the language. All of the students were first motivated to learn a language to either consume more media from that culture, connect with their heritage, or adapt to a new country.

I used this information to create two personas to help guide me through the design journey.


Problems

Based on my primary research, students face three main problems when learning or retaining a language on their own:

1. They’re busy.
2. It’s hard to stay motivated.
3. Lack of access to feedback.

I turned these three problems into necessary functions and created a morphological chart to generate possible solutions and determine the combination that would best solve the users’ problems based on feasibility and effectiveness.

How is Cultura different?

Cultura differentiates itself from other products in the digital language-learning sector in its focus on the WHY of learning a language: connecting to a culture through language and verbal communication. Products like Duolingo and Conjuguemos are amazing at providing active vocabulary and grammar practice, and Cultura can supplement those resources by fitting into the users’ existing lifestyle through its listening aspect, minimizing the time a person needs to set out of their day to learn.

First Iteration

Two success metrics that would indicate how well the design solves the users’ problems are:

1. Task completion rate.
The task completion rate suggests the clarity of the design. I set the success threshold to 92% to be in the top quartile; anything lower than 92% means that there may be significant usability problems.

2. Net Promoter Score.
This metric is a bit more tricky since it depends on the first impression of the product, but it does provide a quantitative measure to determine users' opinions of the product. I set my benchmark NPS to be 0, with an average score of 8. Although 9 would be ideal, I chose 8 to be a buffer.

After conducting 3 usability testing sessions, I compared my findings to the success metrics. The results didn’t quite hit the mark:

1. Task completion rate.
Out of the 15 tasks, 13 were completed correctly. This result of 87% did not meet the benchmark of 92%, but since it was just one task from reaching the goal, I knew there were some minor issues with design clarity and meeting users’ expectations that I should address in the next iteration.

2. Net Promoter Score.
This result just met the benchmark with an NPS of 0 and an average rating of 8. However, this was not a strong indicator because although I reminded the users that their answers wouldn’t hurt my feelings in any way, the users may have still felt uncomfortable letting me know their true thoughts. It would also be difficult to predict if the users would continue using this product from this metric alone, so I would likely use the NPS in a non-personal setting or after the user has used a product for a longer time.

I also gathered my top four insights:

1. The app functionality is unclear upfront.
- 3/3 users were unsure of what to expect of the app.
- “I'm interested in a topic but I’m unsure of what it will be when you click on it.”

2. The app is not beginner-friendly.
- 3/3 users thought the app would be difficult to use for beginners.
- “Titles being in Korean scares me a bit; I don’t know what it means.”
- “Personally I’m not too sure I would use it because I don’t know enough to answer a question.”

3. Lack of trust.
- 2/3 users found it difficult to trust feedback from random users.
- “People evaluating are other users. I don’t know how much they know about the other language...if I’m putting in a lot of effort, it would be nice to have confidence in the person grading it.”

4. Episodes are too long.
- 3/3 users found the number of episodes to be too long, especially in the context of their commutes (15-30 min).

These insights showed that the design did not meet users' expectations. Next time, running through a quick heuristic evaluation before usability testing will help me avoid making the same mistakes. The design also does not consider ALL users — I realized from this session that the biggest differentiating factor between users was not the specific reasons for learning a language but the users' level of expertise in the languages. So, I made another persona to represent language beginners and keep them in mind while doing the second iteration.

Second Iteration

I made several changes to address the main issues:

1. The app functionality is unclear upfront

The users expected an explanation of the app at the beginning or did not fully understand the explanation on the sign up page, so I wanted to make it more clear that the user would be able to listen to listen to episodes in a foreign language and receive feedback on their recorded answers. Since I wanted to make the functionality intuitive, I included a diagnostic test that mimics how the app works to give users a clear expectation of how to use the app. The diagnostic test would also excite the user as they anticipate a result.


2. The app is not beginner-friendly

This point ties in with the first issue. I specified the unique experience each user type (beginner, intermediate, advanced) would have and how Cultura would accomodate for their levels. If the user knows that they are a complete beginner, they can skip the test.


3. Lack of trust

Instead of having feedback from users, the feedback would now be from paid Cultura language experts.


4. Episodes are too long

I decreased the length of episodes to around 15 minutes to accommodate for the amount of time each user was expected to spend in one sitting on the app.

Let’s get down to business

Throughout the design process, I thought about how this product would function with business requirements. How would this app fund itself? Because one main pillar of the app is accessibility, I did not want this app to be paid. Implementing ads in users' target languages would provide some learning value to them while funding the product, making this usually intrusive advertisement component more pleasant. I would have to research more about the business aspect of products to determine if this method is feasible.


Next steps

The second round of usability testing is in progress.
Testing the success of the app would be difficult without creating a working prototype because it is meant to be used over time. If I were to develop the app, these are some KPIs I would use to gauge the product’s success, determined by engagement:

1. Episode completion rate: 35%, based on the average length of Cultura episodes (around 15 minutes)

2. Listen frequency: 7 episodes per week, based on the US average

3. Feedback-to-listen ratio: This data would be important to determine how useful the feedback feature is. Because I couldn’t find a benchmark for this, I would likely do some testing to determine this number.
